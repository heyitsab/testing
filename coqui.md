# The Coquí

## Overview

The **coquí** (*Eleutherodactylus coqui*) is a small tree frog native to Puerto Rico and one of the island's most beloved symbols. Named after its distinctive two-note call — *"ko-KEE"* — the coquí is deeply embedded in Puerto Rican culture and identity.

## Physical Description

- **Size:** 1–2 inches (2.5–5 cm) in length
- **Color:** Ranges from yellow-green and brown to gray, often with darker markings
- **Eyes:** Large, prominent eyes adapted for nighttime activity
- **Feet:** Sticky toe pads that help them climb plants, trees, and smooth surfaces

## Habitat

Coquís thrive in a wide range of environments across Puerto Rico, including:

- Tropical rainforests (such as El Yunque National Forest)
- Suburban gardens and parks
- Coastal areas and scrublands

They prefer moist environments and are often found hiding under leaves, in bromeliads, or in tree bark during the day.

## Behavior and Diet

Coquís are **nocturnal**, becoming active at dusk. They feed on:

- Insects (moths, beetles, ants)
- Spiders
- Small invertebrates

They are sit-and-wait predators, ambushing prey with a quick lunge.

## The Iconic Call

The coquí's call is one of its most distinctive features. The two-note call is produced exclusively by **males**:

- **"Ko"** — a territorial warning to other males
- **"Kee"** — an advertisement call to attract females

The chorus of coquís at night is considered a comforting and nostalgic sound for Puerto Ricans, often described as the "sound of home."

## Reproduction

Unlike most frogs, coquís do **not** have a tadpole stage. Key reproductive facts:

- Females lay small clutches of eggs (10–30) in moist, hidden spots
- The male guards the eggs until they hatch
- Eggs hatch directly into fully formed miniature froglets
- This process is known as **direct development**

## Cultural Significance

The coquí is an enduring symbol of Puerto Rico:

- Featured in art, music, and folklore
- Used as a cultural emblem representing Puerto Rican pride and heritage
- The phrase *"Soy de aquí como el coquí"* ("I am from here like the coquí") is a common expression of Puerto Rican identity

## Conservation Status

Most coquí species are not currently endangered, but habitat loss due to deforestation and urbanization poses a threat. Some of the 17+ species found in Puerto Rico are listed as vulnerable or critically endangered.

## Invasive Range

Coquís have been introduced (accidentally or otherwise) to:

- **Hawaii**, where they are considered an invasive species due to the noise they produce and competition with native wildlife
- Parts of Florida and other regions
