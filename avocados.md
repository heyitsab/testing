# Facts About Avocados

## General

- Avocados are the fruit of the *Persea americana* tree, native to south-central Mexico.
- They are botanically classified as a large berry containing a single seed.
- The most popular variety is the **Hass avocado**, which accounts for over 80% of avocados eaten worldwide.
- Avocado trees are evergreen and can live for hundreds of years.

## Nutrition

- Avocados are rich in **healthy monounsaturated fats**, particularly oleic acid.
- A single avocado contains about 20 different vitamins and minerals.
- They are an excellent source of **potassium** — containing more potassium per gram than bananas.
- Avocados are high in **fiber**, with roughly 7 grams per half fruit.
- They contain vitamins K, C, B5, B6, and folate (B9).
- Despite being high in fat, avocados are cholesterol- and sodium-free.

## History & Culture

- Avocados have been cultivated in Central and South America for at least **5,000 years**.
- The word "avocado" comes from the Nahuatl word *āhuacatl*.
- Spanish conquistadors documented avocados in the 16th century and brought them to Europe.
- They were introduced to the United States in the early 19th century.

## Growing

- Avocado trees require a warm climate and are primarily grown in Mexico, Peru, Indonesia, and California.
- Mexico is the world's largest producer of avocados, supplying roughly **30% of global production**.
- A single avocado tree can produce around 200–300 fruits per year.
- It takes a newly planted avocado tree **3–5 years** to bear fruit.
- Avocados do not ripen on the tree — they only ripen after being harvested.

## Fun Facts

- Avocados are toxic to many animals, including birds, rabbits, and cats.
- The Hass avocado was discovered by a mail carrier named Rudolph Hass, who patented the tree in 1935.
- Avocado is a key ingredient in **guacamole**, a dish with pre-Columbian origins.
- The inside of an avocado pit can be used as a natural ink or dye.
- Avocados are sometimes called "**alligator pears**" because of their bumpy green skin.
